WHICH YERBA MATÉ ARE YOU?

1. Where is Guayaki yerba maté grown?

South America

Guayaki yerba maté is grown in the Atlantic Forests of South America, specifically in Argentina, Paraguay, and Brazil. Guayaki sources their yerba maté from local communities and indigenous peoples who continue to use their traditional methods of cultivation. Guayaki’s yerba maté is fair trade and is grown using regenerative and organic practices.


2. How often is yerba maté harvested?

Every 1-3 Years

Yerba maté is carefully harvested by hand every 1-3 years. Havesters are mindful not to take too many leaves from the plant when harvesting. The leaves harvested for Guayaki are air-dried using a slow process before being ground and aged for 6-12 months. After aging, the yerba maté leaves go through a final process of grinding before being used for tea.


3. How tall do yerba maté trees grow in the wild?

Up to 100 feet

Yerba Maté can grow up to 100 feet in the wild, but cultivated yerba maté only reaches 25 to 60 feet.


4. What kind of plant is yerba maté?

Holly

Yerba Maté is a type of holly indigenous to South America and parts of Asia. Its leaves are anti-inflammatory and high in antioxidants. It contains anti-cancer properties, can help prevent diabetes, and can help increase your metabolism and heart functioning.


5. Which indigenous cultures cultivated, and enjoyed yerba maté?

The Guaraní, Tupí, Jibaro, Quechua, and Kaingang…

The Guaraní, Tupí, Jibaro, Quechua, Kaingang, and others are all known for cultivating and enjoying yerba maté. The plant has played an integral role in the indigenous cultures of the Amazon in what is now Paraguay, Argentina, Brazil, Ecuador, Venezuela, and Colombia. The tea is traditionally drunk socially, often around friends, family, and community during conversation and storytelling. In the Guarani language, yerba maté is called Ka’a, or 'the forest inside the tree.' In the Kaingang language, Jê, it is called Kóguhn, or 'the tree that nurtures/sustains' For these indigenous peoples, yerba maté was a sacred and integral part of their lives and was used in ritual as well as community gatherings. Today, the plant is still used and enjoyed by these communities and is shared across the world.
